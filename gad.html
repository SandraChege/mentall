Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Occasional anxiety is a normal part of life. Many people may worry about things such as health, money, or family problems. But people with GAD feel extremely worried or nervous more frequently about these and other things—even when there is little or no reason to worry about them.

Generalized anxiety disorder produces fear, worry, and a constant feeling of being overwhelmed. Generalized anxiety disorder is characterized by persistent, excessive, and unrealistic worry about everyday things. This worry could be multifocal such as finance, family, health, and the future. It is excessive, difficult to control, and is often accompanied by many non-specific psychological and physical symptoms.

Causes of Generalized Anxiety Disorder 

There are many causes of generalized anxiety disorder and they include: 

  • Traumatic events
  • Genetics
  • Work pressure
  • Financial problems
  • Biological, psychological and social factors

Signs of Generalized anxiety disorder 

There are many signs of generalized anxiety disorder and they include: 

  • Worrying excessively about everyday things
  • Feeling restless and have trouble relaxing
  • Having a hard time concentrating
  • Have trouble falling asleep or staying asleep
  • Have headaches, muscle aches, stomachaches, or unexplained pains
  • Tire easily or feel tired all the time
  • Sweat a lot, feel lightheaded, or feel out of breath

What can I do to manage my generalized anxiety disorder?

You can do the following to help you manage your generalized anxiety disorder:

  • Educate yourself about GAD
  • Have an honest conversation about how you’re feeling with someone you trust.
  • Keep physically active. Develop a routine so that you're physically active most days of the week. Exercise is a powerful stress reducer.
  • Make sleep a priority. Do what you can to make sure you're getting enough sleep to feel rested.
  • Use relaxation techniques. Visualization techniques, meditation and yoga are examples of relaxation techniques that can ease anxiety
  • Eat healthy.  
  • Seek help from a professional counselor 
  • Avoid alcohol and recreational drugs.
  • Quit smoking and cut back or quit drinking coffee.
  • Stick to your treatment plan.  

 

If you're worried about your GAD, it's important to seek help as soon as possible.
