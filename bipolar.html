Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder, formely called maniac depression, is a mental health condition that causes extreme mood swings that include emotional highs (mania or hypomania) and lows (depression) 

When you become depressed, you may feel sad or hopeless and lose interest or pleasure in most activities. When your mood shifts to mania or hypomania(less extreme than mania), you may feel euphoroc, full of energy or unusually irritable. These mood swings can affect sleep, energy, judgement, behavior and ability to think clearly.  

Although bipolar disorder is a lifelong condition, you can be able to manageyour mood swings and other symptoms by following a treatment plan. Bipolar disorder, in most cases, is treated with medications and psychological counselling(psychotherapy)  

Causes of stress 

The exact cause of bipolar disorder is still unknown but some of the causes of bipolar disorder include: 

  • Extreme stress
  • Overwhelming problems
  • Life changing events
  • Genetic and chemical factors

Symptoms of bipolar disorder

Some of the symptoms of bipolar disorder include:  

  • Extreme high self-esteem
  • Need less sleep
  • Very talkative 
  • Racing thoughts
  • Fatigue
  • Feeling worthless
  • Feeling down or depressed
  • Loss of interest in things you usually enjoy
  • Appetite changes with weight loss or gain
  • Doing things that may have negative consequences such as big shopping sprees
  • Very focused on achieving goals
  • Easily distracted

What can I do to manage bipolar disorder?

You can do the following to help you manage bipolar disorder:

  • Reduce stress at work and at home
  • Stay away from drugs and alcohol
  • Maintain a stable sleep pattern
  • Find out what triggers your mood episode so you can know when it is likely to pop up
  • Eat healthily and exercise
  • Talk to a trusted friend, adult, or family 
  • Seek help from a professional counselor 
  • Engage in copying skills to control symptoms or reduce your risk for a mood episode. These activities should help you feel calm, stay connected to others, and practice healthy habits.
  • Spend time with people who make you feel safe and good about yourself. They may be your family or friends. This can boost your moods and keep you energized.
  • Continue treatment as advised by your healthcare provider

 

If you're worried about your bipolar disorder, it's important to seek help as soon as possible.
